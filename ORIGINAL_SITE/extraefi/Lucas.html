Plug-N-Play ECU for the RV8 Lucas 14CUX

Let's Start at the Basics:

All Electronic Fuel Injection (EFI) systems basically work in the same way. They use various combinations of sensors to monitor positions, temperatures, exhaust gases, engine load and engine speed to calculate how much fuel will be put into the engine and, more often than not, when to ignite that fuel.

Fuel:

If we consider the Lucas systems (14CUX and 4CU) as used on the TVR RV8 engines, then we don’t have any ignition control, the ECU is only concerned with fuel. The amount of fuel it puts into the cylinder is controlled by how long the injector is open for. The ECU reads the signals from the sensors and uses this information to determine a ‘position’ in the data tables within its memory. These tables contain values for injector opening times, as the state of the engine changes, so does the amount of fuel being delivered. In order to understand how the system works, we need to know what the sensors are, and how the signals are used.

Air Flow Meter. (Flapper / Hotwire)

Love them or hate them, these are used by most manufacturers as a way of measuring the air flow into the engine and thereby determining the amount of fuel needed to be mixed with that air. Early versions of the air flow meter consisted of a sprung ‘flap’ placed in the inlet air flow. This flap is ‘pushed’ open by the air being drawn into the engine. Although fairly rudimentary, these systems did at least offer some indication of the load that the engine was under, and controlled the fuelling system in most situations. Later versions, such as the ‘Hotwire’ air flow meters, were much better at determining ‘Air Mass’ passing through them. The advantage of knowing the mass, rather than just volume, of air passing through the sensor is that air density, which affects the combustion characteristics of the air, is taken into account. This means that, in theory at least, the engine will be optimally fuelled in all atmospheric conditions.

Inlet Air Temperature Sensor (IAT) (Built into the AFM on the Lucas ECU)

This sensor changes resistance with temperature (increase in temperature - decreases resistance). It is used to calculate the density of the air coming into the engine. The hotter the air, the thinner it will be (less dense) so we will need less fuel. The colder the air the more dense it is, so we need more fuel to maintain the same mixture. The ECU will use the temperature as part of a calculation to work out how much to alter the fuelling to allow for the actual air density. This formula is known as Boules law, you may remember it from Physics at school. The actual calculation isn’t important, as long as you appreciate its function.

Coolant Temperature Sensor (CTS)

This sensor, like the IAT, changes resistance with temperature (increase in temperature - decreases resistance) When an engine is cold, the inlet walls soak up more fuel than they will when hot and the engine will also need to run a little richer for the fuel to mix well in the air. So the ECU will use a lookup table to work out how much extra fuel needs to be added while running during the ‘warm-up’ period. This is usually a percentage addition made to the load tables mentioned earlier. As an example, at a temperature of 45 degrees C, we might determine that the engine requires an extra 10% fuel delivery, compared to the full running temperature value that is stored in the fuel table. We therefore produce a second table (warm-up) which adds that 10% to the calculation before the signal is sent to the injectors. Because we use the optimal running temperature of the engine as a 100% reference point, the 45 degree value for the fuel table will be referred to as 110%. The coolant sensor is also used to work out the amount of fuel injected into the engine during cranking, in fact, for fuelling, all the ECU will care about, during cranking, is the coolant temperature.

Throttle Position Sensor (TPS)

This is a simple ‘potentiometer’ that varies resistance as it rotates. The 3 pinned sensor is fed with a reference voltage, usually 5V, and as it is rotated gives a variable voltage output, 0V when the throttle is closed, 5V when the throttle is fully open.

The ECU can use this sensor as an indication of engine load and also as a measure of how fast the throttle has been moved. Although variables such as air mass and engine vacuum are not taken into consideration, the degree to which the throttle is opened is a reasonably good indication of the load that the engine is under. On the TVR500 engines, I believe that the TPS is used once the AFM reaches its limit, so switching from AFM to TPS as the load scale for the fuelling at high RPM.

Exhaust Gas Oxygen – EGO (Lambda Sensor)

The Lucas Hotwire system, like most other manufacturers, uses a narrow band lambda probe. This probe is mounted in the exhaust gas stream and measures the amount of un-burnt fuel remaining in the exhaust gas. A reference figure for ideal combustion is known as the ‘Stoichimetric’ mixture and is indicated by an Air Fuel Ratio (AFR) of 14.7:1 or a ‘Lambda’ value of 1. If the exhaust AFR is less that 14.7:1 (or Lambda below 1), the mixture is termed as ‘Rich’ and inversely, if above 14.7 (or Lambda above 1) then it is lean. This may sound complicated, but is actually relatively straightforward when viewed.

The ECU uses this information to decide whether to increase the fuel a little or decrease it, depending if the probe is telling it its rich or lean. The fuelling will be corrected approximately twice or three times a second, so it is constantly trying to maintain a target AFR. This is known as “Exhaust Gas Oxygen (EGO) correction”. There will be a limit to how much the ECU can trim this correction, probably 10-15% in the Lucas 14CUX.

When ‘narrowband’ sensors are used, the correction is only carried out at low engine loads and speeds. This is because narrowband sensors, as their names suggest, can only measure a very narrow ‘window’ of air fuel ratio. The complication here is that a performance engine tends to run best at AFR’s approaching 12.5:1, at least when under heavy loads, and therefore a narrowband sensor is pretty well useless unless the car is cruising or running at very low loads.

As a side note the more modern ‘Wideband’ sensors are far more preferable as they measure a far wider spectrum of AFR and can therefore be used to attain correct AFR under many different conditions.

A Wideband lambda setup (TechEdge, Innovates) can detect over the full mixture range, 10-20AFR and should be thought of as an essential tool for tuning any performance engine.

That’s all the sensors covered for the Lucas setup, so now we can move onto the devices it controls:


 

Fuel Pump

The High Pressure Fuel Pump is gravity fed and is mounted ‘inline’ between the fuel tank and the engine. In order to get the fuel into the engine, an injector requires a volume of fuel to be available at a certain pressure. If the pressure is too low, the injectors will not ‘spray’ the fuel, it will just dribble through. If the pressure is too high, the injector will not be able to stop the fuel from flowing past it and the injector will be forced open. When the pressure is correct the injector will produce a well ‘atomised’ mist of fuel that will be easily ‘vaporised’ as it is pulled into the engine. Pressure in the system can vary from manufacturers but 32psi - 45 psi is roughly correct. 36 psi is the pre-set pressure for the RV8 EFI engines. The fuel pressure is actually controlled by a Fuel Pressure Regulator which is pre-set to allow a pressure of 36 psi to be maintained within the fuel rail. A pressure reference pipe (vacuum pipe) attached to the plenum chamber, ‘holds’ the pressure regulator until the Manifold Actual Pressure (MAP) reduces (this happens when the throttle is opened) and then allows the pressure to rise to 36 psi. This is used as a means of enriching the engine slightly when the throttle is opened and also helps to maintain fuel pressure in the rail as the injectors open for longer. This is important for the ECU, as it needs a constant pressure to ensure that any given opening time will allow the same amount of fuel to flow under all conditions.

The Fuel Return Line is not under pressure, it is simply the excess fuel that the pump has supplied that the regulator didn't need to maintain its required pressure. Some Fuel Rails are large in diameter to allow for a greater volume of fuel to be held behind the injector.

Injectors

The injector is basically a solenoid, or electrically operated valve, which opens when a voltage is allowed to pass through it. A 12V feed is applied to one pin and the ECU switches the other pin to ground in order open the solenoid. As the solenoid opens, fuel is allowed to flow through the injector and into the inlet port. There is no positive or negative to an injector so they can be connected either way round.


The longer they are opened for, the more fuel passes through. There is a limit to how much fuel can be delivered by any given injector, this referred to as the injector flow rate. This is important as injectors become less accurate as they open and close too fast. The fastest effective speed that an injector can open, close and then open again is referred to as the Duty Cycle. The maximum recommended duty cycle to maintain accurate fuel delivery is 85%, if the injectors have to ‘work’ harder than this, it is time to get bigger injectors!

As explained above, the fuel pressure is regulated so if it is opened for say 1.5mS, the same amount of fuel will flow from the injector under all load conditions. An injector is rated by its flow in either lbs/hr or cc/min, this is usually measured with a fuel pressure of 43PSI.

1 lb/hr ~ 10.5cc/min

A rough guide to selecting injectors is:

Injector Size = (Horse Power * BSFC) / (#Injectors * Duty Cycle)
BSFC = brake specific fuel consumption. This is usually between 0.42 and 0.58 at wide open throttle. Normally aspirated engines with efficient combustion processes are at the lower end of the BSFC scale ~0.45, where as a supercharged engine tend to be towards the higher end ~0.55.
Using ‘bigger’, or indeed ‘smaller’, injectors within a management system controlled by a ‘Standard’ ECU is not recommended. The standard ECU will be expecting a certain amount of fuel to be delivered by each injector pulse, if the injector delivers more or less than this, the engine will be running rich or lean. In a ‘mappable’ ECU system, injector sizes are often changed to optimise alterations made to the engine.

There is a misconception that you can simply increase fuel pressure to increase your injectors flow rate. Fuel flow doesn't increase at the same rate as pressure, flow increases at the square root of fuel pressure, so you'll need a massive increase in fuel pressure to gain much from an injector. There is a limit that your pump and pipes and injectors can take, increase fuel pressure too far and the injectors will not shut properly, the pattern will change and pipe work is stressed.


Idle Valves

When an engine is cold, cylinder pressures and piston clearances tend to be a little less than optimal. This means that cold engines need to run a little faster, and generally a little richer at idle in order to run smoothly. This need is serviced by an idle air control valve (stepper motor) in a Lucas Hotwire system. The idle engine speed that the ECU will try to maintain will depend directly on the coolant sensor. Once up to normal running temperature the idle valve will be nearly closed and will then only be used as an anti stall device if the engine speed drops too low.


On the Flapper engines, the idle is controlled using an Extra Air Valve, which is basically a bi-metal strip that opens when it’s cold, allowing air to flow into the intake and closes slowly as it warms up. The Flapper ECU had no control over this item.

Ignition:

Timing

The fuel air mixture within the combustion chamber of a petrol engine burns at a specific rate. This rate is determined by a number of factors (Fuel Octane, cylinder pressure, Oxygen content etc.) but for most us running normal grade pump fuel and normally aspirated engines the rate is fixed. As an engine runs at higher speed we need to allow it a few more degrees of rotation to achieve full combustion and therefore make the most of each ‘burn’. If the spark were to be discharged at the same degree of rotation at any engine speed, the fuel would not have time to burn when the engine ran faster. We achieve this by adjusting the spark ‘timing’. Timing is measured in degrees of crankshaft rotation before Top Dead Centre (the point at which a piston stops its upward motion and begins its journey back down the cylinder). This ‘ignition timing’ is controlled via a sensor within the distributor and the Lucas ECU has no control over this what-so-ever. This is fundamentally the same system that was used on engines up to the early 80’s, whereby a pair of weights inside the distributor body, are thrown outwards with the centrifugal force as the speed of the distributor shaft increases. This in turn, moves the plate that the sensor is bolted to by a few degrees, which means the coil fires earlier as engine speed increases. The movement is limited to around 20 or so degrees.

There is also a vacuum diaphragm connected to the plate. This adds more advance under light throttle conditions, allowing the ECU to run the engine leaner under those conditions.

The sensor will trigger the signal amplifier unit, which is connected to the coil.

Coil

The coil is a relatively simple device which has 2 windings inside it. The central winding is the Primary and then there’s a Secondary winding wound around that. The secondary has 100's of times more windings than the primary. This then acts in a similar way to a 'step up' transformer.
The Primary winding has a 12V ignition feed to it and the other side (-ve) is grounded by the amplifier. When the -ve side is grounded the coil charges up until it is saturated (reaches its maximum current) this is known as the dwell time. The -ve side of the primary is then suddenly opened by the amplifier. As the primary voltage collapses it creates a massive magnetic field that in turn induces a huge voltage to flow into the secondary winding (due to the very large number of turns in the winding).
This voltage is then sent to the distributor and, depending on the position of the ‘rotor arm’ to a spark plug. The secondary winding's voltage is ‘searching’ for a ground and, if everything is well insulated, the nearest ground will be on the tip of the spark plug where it will jump across to create a spark. The larger the spark plug gap, the larger the voltage needs to be to jump across it.

Summary of the 14CUX (and 4CU)

These are my opinions, and let’s face it, I am a likely to be a little biased, so others will have a different spin on it. There is nothing major to worry about as far as fuel goes, it all works fine, not the most efficient or modern setup by a long shot, but it works, up to a point:

If you change something in the engine, e.g. different specification camshaft or increased capacity then you may start to have fuelling issues. If you fit a blower or anything that increases power, then you will definitely have issues with the standard ECU. If you have a TVR 500 engine, then your MAF will hit its maximum output long before the engine does. So again, this is an issue. The fix by TVR was to switch over to TPS as the AFM peaks. This is fine, but the AFM may as well not be there as it becomes a restriction once its maximum flow rate has been reached.

It is possible to swap the EPROM inside most of the Lucas 14CUX ECU's but this would be what's known as a “best guess” map, which isn't a real answer. I've tuned a lot of RV8 engine's and never ended up with the same map inside any of them, so a “best guess” EPROM is going to be like a lottery.

The hotwire ECU can be re-programmed to match your engine, this would be the best solution for keeping the 14CUX and having an engine running its optimum fuelling, but due to the fact that only a couple of people in the world have the equipment to do it, its not going to be an easy solution. Remember that if any more changes are made to the engine, it would mean going through this process again.

The technology within the Lucas is well past it, electronics moves so very quickly and the late 80s was hundreds of years ago, as far as technology is concerned (think ZX Spectrum). This means that modern ECU’s can calculate the fuelling far more accurately, control the injectors with far more precision and can carry out far more functions than Lucas could have ever imagined.

So what does that mean in the real world? Let’s consider the fuel flow from an injector. With all else being equal, the engines’ emissions and power are all related to how much fuel enters the cylinder, how it burns and how it is removed. Controlling this fuel accurately is critical to efficiency and the power, as its all down to how long those injectors are opened for by the ECU.

Take an example of an injector opening time of 2.0mSec at say 1500RPM (cruising)

We will ignore opening time for now (time taken for the injector’s internals to move to allow flow)

Let’s say it’s giving a mixture of 15.3 AFR. We ideally want 14.7 AFR.

So we add 0.1mS to the opening time.

Whilst I have no proof of this, I suspect that’s about as small an opening step that the Lucas can provide given the technology around its development.

So we have increased the fuel flow by 0.1mS/2.0mS * 100 = 5%

So the AFR will now alter by 5%, so it will be 14.5AFR, rather than 15.3AFR.

This means we can only step in 0.8AFR jumps at low engine speeds.

Where as a modern ECU can control the injectors down to 0.001mS and even lower, so theoretically in 0.008AFR steps given the same figures as above.

Let’s now consider the ignition. Well the TVR setup has no technology in that what so ever, which is good as it means there’s a ton of improvements that we can make to it!

The High Tension voltage is generated by the coil, which is switched on by an amplifier, the length it is turned on for is what’s known as “dwell time”. This is how long the coil is charged for before it is discharged. The discharge generates the spark, so unless the coil reaches its maximum charge (saturation) we will lose power on the HT side resulting in a weak spark.

Well a standard ignition coil needs approx 4.0mS to charge and 1.0mS to discharge, a total of 5mS. On a distributor based V8, this has to happen 4 times for every engine rotation (4 stroke engine)

So that’s 5mS x 4 = 20mS to fully charge and discharge the coil each revolution.

20mS is the same time it takes an engine to rotate at 3000RPM.

So any engine speed above 3000RPM and the coil is not reaching saturation, therefore the spark is starting to weaken.

Now think of 6000RPM, that’s 10mS for 4 sparks, or 2.5mS for each spark. Take the discharge time away and there’s only 1.5mS to charge the coil. It’s simply impossible to fully charge it in that time, so the result is a weaker spark.

Whilst we are talking about the ignition, the timing accuracy is all down to a chain and gear driven sensor which has backlash and other inaccuracies inherent to them.

Aftermarket ECU’s

A MegaSquirt MS2 or MS3 ECU has the timing accuracy of 0.1deg and the fuelling is theoretically capable of up to 0.16uS resolution. The MS3 has two processors, one calculating the ignition (which is time critical), the other calculating the fuelling, etc.

Whilst you may say this is over kill, it allows the ECU to be designed to do much more, for example: Sequential fuelling, Sequential ignition, Mappable fuel timing, Knock control, Launch control, Variable Valve Timing (VVT), Shift lights, Fan outputs, CAN databus link, Switchable maps, Wideband Lambda correction, EGT measuring, Boost control, etc, etc, etc. None of this could be done using the Lucas system.

So as you can see, the Lucas system works on a standard engine, but compare it to any modern ECU and its short falls soon become apparent. If you have any modifications carried out on your engine, e.g. cam, different size injectors, heads ported, etc, then the Lucas will need either re-programming or it simply won’t fuel it efficiently for those modifications. This is where an aftermarket ECU comes into its own, as they can be mapped so the fuelling is correct for any engine with just about any modification.

Throw in mappable ignition control (as fitted to virtually all aftermarket ECU’s) and you’ll have a much more driveable engine that is more efficient and probably give out more power over all or at least part of the rev range.

Wasted spark is the most common upgrade for the ignition system, using a MegaSquirt ECU, this allows the coils to fully saturate at 6000rpm and beyond, so no more weak sparks at the plugs.

Most people’s comments I hear from MegaSquirt fitted cars are how much better and nicer it is to drive. A lot of this comes from combining fuel and ignition control in an ECU, as the two work together. Fuelling is directly related to ignition timing, so being able to map both works out for a much better setup.
